MARKET RATIOS

Reading the market in ratios

Cross-market ratios cut through the noise of individual price moves and show what's happening between two assets. When silver is cheap relative to gold, when stocks are expensive relative to gold, when the US is outperforming the rest of the world — these long-running ratios tell you which regime the market is in.

The Joseph Indicator

A McClellan-style breadth oscillator, computed from the daily return differential between the equal-weight S&P (RSP) and the cap-weight S&P (SPY). Positive readings mean breadth is accelerating — the broad market is beating the megacaps. Negative readings mean breadth is deteriorating — the megacaps are carrying the index.

How to read this

The Joseph Indicator uses the same shape as the McClellan Oscillator (short-EMA minus long-EMA), but replaces the McClellan’s NYSE advance/decline count with a breadth pulse we can actually compute from public data: the daily return of RSP minus the daily return of SPY. RSP holds all 500 S&P stocks equally, SPY is cap-weighted. When the average stock beats the average dollar, breadth is broadening. When megacaps beat the average stock, breadth is narrowing.

Formula: pulse = 100 × (RSP_return − SPY_return). Joseph = EMA19(pulse) − EMA39(pulse). EMA lengths mirror the classic McClellan (19 and 39 days).

  • Above the zero line: breadth accelerating. Broad market outrunning the megacaps — healthy tape.
  • Below the zero line: breadth deteriorating. Megacaps carrying the index — narrow rally, stealth weakness.
  • Zero-line cross from below: fresh breadth rally — historically a bullish setup after a market bottom.
  • Zero-line cross from above: breadth breaking down — watch for broader trouble.
  • Above the 90th percentile: unusually strong breadth — often near bear-market recovery lows.
  • Below the 10th percentile: unusually narrow tape — often coincides with market tops or panic-narrow moments.

Historical extremes: the biggest positive readings show up right after major bear-market lows (early 2009, mid-2020, late 2022) when the broad market rebounded harder than megacaps. Deep negatives cluster around narrow-rally moments dominated by a small handful of leaders. Use the range picker above to zoom in on any specific regime.

How to read this

The Gold/Silver ratio measures how many ounces of silver it takes to buy one ounce of gold. It's a classic contrarian tool in the precious-metals world: when the ratio is unusually high, silver is cheap relative to gold and has historically outperformed on the reversal; when it's unusually low, silver is expensive and gold tends to catch up.

  • Above 90: Silver deeply undervalued vs gold. Rare — happened in the 2008 crisis, 2020 COVID, 2016 mining crash.
  • 60–80: Normal historical range. No strong signal.
  • Below 50: Silver expensive vs gold. Historically the setup where silver's rally has run out of room.

Extremes: 2020 COVID crash pushed the ratio to ~125 (all-time high). 1980 Hunt-brothers silver squeeze pushed it to ~15 (all-time low).

How to read this

This ratio compares the S&P 500 index level to the gold price per ounce. It's a barometer of investor risk appetite: how much you'd have to pay in ounces of gold to buy one "share" of the S&P 500.

  • Rising: stocks growing faster than gold — risk-on, corporate confidence, positive real growth expectations.
  • Falling: gold outpacing stocks — defensive positioning, inflation hedging, or geopolitical stress.

Historical extremes: 1999 dot-com peak: 5.5 (stocks wildly overvalued vs gold). 1980 stagflation trough: ~0.2 (gold expensive, stocks depressed). The ratio has trended lower recently — even though the S&P is at record highs, gold's bull run has outpaced equity gains, compressing the ratio.

Gold / Oil ratio

Median since 2000 –

Loading gold/oil ratio…

How to read this

How many barrels of oil one ounce of gold buys. Because oil is the world's growth commodity and gold is its defensive one, this ratio flags when the two decouple — usually because something in the global economy is breaking.

  • Rising sharply: oil crashing faster than gold. Deflationary shock, demand destruction, or recession fear. Historically peaks near recession lows.
  • Falling sharply: oil rallying faster than gold. Inflation, supply shock, or reflation trade. Common in commodity booms.
  • Near median: normal risk regime, no strong signal.

Historical extremes: April 2020 (COVID + negative oil day): ~90+ all-time high. 2008 GFC oil crash: ~25. 1986 oil-price collapse: ~30+. 2008 pre-GFC oil peak ($147/bbl): ~6.

US vs. International (VTI / VEU)

Current –
Median since 2007 –

Loading US/international ratio…

How to read this

This ratio compares the price of the Vanguard Total US Stock Market ETF (VTI) to the Vanguard FTSE All-World ex-US ETF (VEU). It's the cleanest one-line snapshot of who's winning between US stocks and everyone else — a barometer of global equity leadership.

  • Rising: US stocks outperforming international — earnings, tech dominance, dollar strength, or all three usually driving it.
  • Falling: International outperforming US — often coincides with a weaker dollar, commodity booms, or US mega-cap valuations coming down.
  • Flat/rangebound: No clear regime, roughly equal returns.

Regime history: the 2000s were a "lost decade" for US stocks — international outperformed and the ratio fell. From roughly 2010 onward, US mega-cap tech drove the ratio steadily higher, hitting new records year after year through the mid-2020s. Sustained turns in this line have historically marked multi-year leadership shifts.

SPY / XLF — S&P 500 vs large financials

Current –
Median since 2003 –

Loading SPY/XLF ratio…

How to read this

This ratio compares the S&P 500 ETF (SPY) to the Financial Select Sector SPDR (XLF) — large US banks and financials. When the ratio rises, the broad market is beating financials; when it falls, financials are outperforming (usually because rate hikes or a strong yield curve are helping bank earnings).

  • Rising sharply: financials underperforming the market — often a credit stress or recession-fear signal.
  • Falling: financials leading — classic mid-cycle setup, healthy net-interest margins.
  • Near median: no strong sector rotation, financials tracking the broader market.

Regime examples: 2008–2009 GFC: XLF crashed harder than SPY, ratio spiked to record highs. 2013–2018 rate-hike cycle: XLF outperformed, ratio drifted lower. March 2023 (SVB collapse): brief spike as regional bank fear bled into large financials.

Median since 2006 –

Loading SPY/KRE ratio…

How to read this

Same shape as SPY/XLF but scoped to regional banks (SPDR S&P Regional Banking ETF, KRE). Regional banks live and die by the yield curve, commercial real-estate exposure, and deposit stability — so this ratio is often the earliest tell that something is breaking in the banking system.

  • Rising sharply: regional banks underperforming — the classic setup before / during a banking crisis.
  • Falling: regional banks leading — healthy credit environment, steep yield curve.

Extremes: March 2020 COVID crash: KRE fell twice as hard as SPY, ratio spiked. March 2023 SVB / Signature / First Republic collapses: sharpest single-month move ever in this series. A watch-list ratio when the yield curve is flat or inverted.

SPY / EEM — US vs emerging markets

Current –
Median since 2003 –

Loading SPY/EEM ratio…

How to read this

How US equities are doing against emerging markets (iShares MSCI Emerging Markets ETF, EEM). This is the classic dollar-cycle chart: EM tends to win when the dollar is weakening and commodities are rising, and US tends to win when the dollar is strong and tech leads.

  • Rising: US outperforming EM — strong dollar, US mega-cap tech leadership, or EM-specific stress (China, Russia, Turkey).
  • Falling: EM outperforming — weaker dollar, commodity booms, cheaper EM valuations catching up.

Regime history: 2003–2010 was an EM decade — the ratio fell as commodity-driven EM economies (China, Brazil, Russia) surged. 2011–present has been a US decade — mega-cap tech drove the ratio higher year after year, punctuated by brief EM rallies during dollar-weak periods.

How to read this

How the S&P 500 is doing against China (iShares MSCI China ETF, MCHI, which tracks the mainland & Hong Kong-listed large caps). Historically the single largest driver of the EM ratio, isolated here on its own.

  • Rising sharply: China underperforming — often regulatory crackdowns, real-estate stress, or geopolitical friction.
  • Falling: China outperforming — policy stimulus, easing tensions, or a value-rotation trade.

Regime shift: the ratio was roughly range-bound 2011–2020. From late 2020 onward it broke sharply higher on the tech regulatory crackdown, Evergrande / real-estate crisis, and delisting-fear headlines. Watch for sustained downturns as a contrarian sign that China is bottoming.

XLY / XLP — Discretionary vs Staples

Current –
Median since 1998 –

Loading XLY/XLP ratio…

How to read this

The classic risk-on / risk-off ratio for the US consumer. XLY holds discretionary spenders (Amazon, Tesla, Home Depot, McDonald’s); XLP holds the staples (Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola, Walmart). When the ratio rises, people are spending on wants over needs — typically strong-economy behavior. When it falls, money rotates to defensive staples — recession-fear behavior.

  • Rising: risk-on rotation, healthy consumer, expansion phase.
  • Falling: defensive rotation, recession fear, contraction phase.
  • Sharp downturn: often precedes broader market weakness by weeks to months.

Long-term drift: The ratio has trended steadily higher since the 2009 GFC lows, mostly because Amazon and Tesla dominate XLY. Focus on medium-term trend changes rather than the absolute level. This same ratio also appears on the Consumer page in a different framing.

Current –
Median since 2000 –

Loading gold/dollar ratio…

How to read this

Gold is priced in dollars, so the two move against each other by definition — but the relationship isn’t always tight. When both are rising (a “dollar-and-gold” regime), it usually signals stress in the global financial system: everyone flees to hard assets AND US-dollar cash at the same time. This ratio isolates gold’s real strength after removing dollar moves.

  • Rising: gold outpacing the dollar — classic inflation-hedge / safe-haven demand, weakening-dollar regime.
  • Falling: dollar strengthening faster than gold rises (or gold falling as dollar rallies) — deflationary / strong-dollar regime.
  • Both rising together: read the note — the ratio can be flat while both assets rally, which is the “financial stress” setup.

Regime examples: 2003–2011 was a huge run for the ratio — gold went from ~$300 to $1900 while the dollar mostly weakened. 2011–2015 saw a sharp reversal (dollar rally, gold correction). 2020–present: the ratio broke to new all-time highs as gold set records even against a relatively firm dollar.
